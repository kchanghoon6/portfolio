2026 Wharton High School Data Science Competition

Student Team Leader · team of 3 Wharton Global Youth Program (WSABI) Feb–Mar 2026 github.com/kchanghoon6/WHSDSC_2026

A team-based sports-analytics competition run by the Wharton Sports Analytics & Business Initiative and the Wharton Global Youth Program. As Student Team Leader, I led a three-person team that stepped into the role of analytics staff for a simulated World Hockey League, predicting tournament outcomes from a full season of game data.

The challenge

We were given one simulated WHL season — 32 teams and 82 games each, for 1,312 games — where every game is broken down by the home and away teams' offensive lines and defensive pairings. The prompt set four tasks: (1a) power-rank the 32 teams and predict first-round matchup win probabilities, (1b) rank the top 10 teams by offensive-line quality disparity, (1c) communicate that disparity in a single visualization, and (1d) document the methodology.

What we built

  • Power rankings & win probabilities: a Poisson GLM estimating each team's attack and defensive-allowance effects to produce expected goals per matchup, then Round-1 home-win probabilities from the Skellam (goal-differential) distribution.
  • Offensive-line quality disparity: an expected-goals rate (xG/60) for each offensive line, with a first-line-to-second-line ratio per team; regression-based rate estimates reduced usage and opponent confounding, and bootstrap confidence intervals flagged which disparities were robust.
  • Visualization: a single scatter plot relating team strength to offensive-line disparity, labelling a readable subset of teams and marking whether each team's 95% CI crosses the no-disparity threshold of 1.0.
  • Evaluation: model performance assessed with log loss and Brier score under 5-fold cross-validation, plus pipeline QA checks (expected row counts, valid joins, output bounds) for reproducibility.

My role & outcome

As Student Team Leader I coordinated the three-person team and owned the submission and analysis pipeline (Python). Our team participated in the competition and did not advance past Phase 1; the full code is published on GitHub.
